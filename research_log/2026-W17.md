## Paper 1 — PRIORITY ★★★

**Contact-Grounded Policy (CGP): Dexterous Visuotactile Policy with Generative Contact Grounding**
[arXiv:2603.05687](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05687) · Submitted 9 Mar 2026 · Real-robot validation ✓

**Relevance** Q5 (visuotactile encoder design), H6 (contact-consistency loss → more object-agnostic policies)
**Source pass** Citation-Graph (cites ViTacFormer / HORA lineage) + Keyword Sweep

### (a) Q# / H# touched
- **Q5 directly:** presents a novel fusion architecture for visuotactile policy that is explicitly contact-consistent.
- **H6 probe:** CGP *is* the architecture H6 anticipates — a contact-consistency loss that supervises the coupling between robot state and tactile predictions, then converts that coupling into executable targets for a compliance controller. This paper either confirms or refutes H6 empirically.

### (b) What is genuinely new
Prior visuotactile policies (ViTacFormer, FBI, GelFusion) treat tactile as a concatenated observation — the policy sees tactile and produces actions independently. CGP introduces a two-stage architecture:

1. A **conditional diffusion model** operating in a compressed latent space that jointly predicts *coupled* trajectories of future robot state **and** future tactile feedback — neither is predicted independently. This forces the model to learn the causal link between actuation intent and expected contact.
2. A **learned contact-consistency mapping** (a separate trainable head) that converts a predicted (state, tactile) pair into an executable target for a compliance controller. The compliance controller then closes the loop at real hardware frequency.

The critical structural difference from concatenation: the model's action generator never directly outputs joint positions. It outputs (state, tactile) pairs in latent space, and the consistency mapping does the inversion. This means the policy is blind to Deform Map / Digit360 specifics at the action level — contact geometry is abstracted into the latent coupling.

**Real-robot evidence:** Evaluated on 4-finger Allegro V5 with Digit360 fingertip sensors. Tasks span in-hand manipulation, delicate grasping, and tool use. Outperforms visuomotor and visuotactile diffusion-policy baselines across all tasks. A simulated 5-finger Tesollo hand with whole-hand tactile arrays was also used, showing the method is not sensor-specific.

### (c) Decision implication — what changes in YOUR Isaac Lab pipeline next week if this paper is right

CGP's key enabling claim is that the compliance controller removes the need for the policy to model the Deform Map structure directly — so if you port this to Sharpa, your encoder design question (Q5: CNN vs ViT-tiny vs visuotactile fusion) partially collapses: **the encoder only needs to compress deformation fields into a latent that supports (state, deformation) joint prediction; it does not need to directly invert contact geometry into joint targets.**

**Concrete Isaac Lab changes:**
- Add a `TactileSimPlugin` wrapper (using the Isaac Lab SDF-contact interface) to simulate contact deformation fields at inference frequency. CGP's fast tactile sim uses **parallel forward kinematics** to compute sensor-object distances — implement this as a new observation term in `ObservationManager` keyed `deform_contact_field`.
- Replace the current student policy's action head with a (state, contact) prediction head. Student still gets trained via privileged teacher (RMA-family), but the student's output is a *coupled prediction* rather than a direct joint target. The compliance controller maps this to physical joint commands.
- Set compliance controller stiffness Kp to **≤ 5 Nm/rad** (soft regime) during CGP-style training — higher stiffness will break the contact-consistency mapping because it removes the physical compliance the mapping relies on.
- **Metric to watch:** contact force RMS error between predicted and real tactile imprint at the Deform Map resolution. Target: < 15% across all five fingertips on a held-out object set.

### (d) Failure mode to probe first
CGP's compliance controller does all contact force regulation. **If Sharpa's PD gains are too stiff (or tendon slack causes abrupt force steps), the consistency mapping will overfit to the specific compliance profile used in training and will not generalise.** Probe: run CGP-style training at three compliance levels (soft / nominal / stiff), measure sim2real transfer success rate. If success degrades monotonically with stiffness, the Deform Map modality is coupling to control dynamics, not to object geometry — and H6 is more fragile than stated.

## Paper 2 — PRIORITY ★★★

**Closing the Reality Gap: Zero-Shot Sim-to-Real Deployment for Dexterous Force-Based Grasping and Manipulation**
[arXiv:2601.02778](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.02778) · Submitted 9 Jan 2026, v2 Jan 22 2026 · Real-robot validation ✓ · ByteDance Seed

**Relevance** Q3 (sim2real + RMA quantification), Q2 (single-skill mastery)
**Source pass** Keyword Sweep (14-day window missed; surfaced via Citation-Graph from DeXtreme)

> **Note:** Publication date is Jan 2026 — 13 weeks ago, outside the 8-week Citation-Graph window. Flagged here because it was not in any prior log and the practical content density warrants exception. If this was already reviewed in an unreported log, discard.

### (a) Q# / H# touched
- **Q3(a)(b)(c):** Provides the most concrete sim2real cookbook published for multi-finger dexterous RL as of W17. Directly relevant to narrowing DR ranges (Q3b) and teacher→student distillation (Q3c).
- **Q2:** Claims first zero-shot sim2real demonstration of *controllable grasp force tracking* + in-hand reorientation on a 5-DOF hand — both trained purely in sim, zero real-world fine-tuning.

### (b) What is genuinely new
Three distinct contributions, each individually adoptable:

1. **Fast tactile sim via parallel FK:** Instead of physics-based contact simulation (expensive, fragile), they compute tactile signals by computing minimum distance between a dense virtual sensor array and the object mesh via batched forward kinematics. Runs at ~10k step/s in Isaac Gym. The sim2real gap for tactile is dramatically smaller because they are not simulating soft-body deformation — they simulate *distance*, which maps directly to Deform Map's deformation magnitude.

2. **Current-to-torque calibration (no torque sensors):** A data-driven mapping from motor current → joint torque, collected offline from a test rig. This eliminates the need for torque sensors at both training and deployment. Critically, the calibration error is then *randomised* during training, so the policy learns to be robust to calibration drift.

3. **Actuator dynamics randomisation of non-ideal effects:** Specifically randomises backlash (±0.5°), torque–velocity saturation curves, and stiction — not just friction and mass. This matches the failure modes in high-DOF tendon-driven and direct-drive hands far better than standard DR.

**Real-robot evidence:** Zero-shot deployment on a 12-DOF 5-finger hand. Task 1: controllable grasp force tracking (specify target grasp force; hand achieves it). Task 2: in-hand object reorientation. Both tasks succeed without any real-world fine-tuning. The paper also shows that lower autotune MSE (a system ID metric) correlates with higher sim2real success rate, providing a concrete proxy metric for Q3(a).

### (c) Decision implication — what changes in YOUR Isaac Lab pipeline next week if this paper is right

**Highest immediate ROI item:** Replace the current tactile observation in your Isaac Lab env with the **parallel-FK distance field** approach. This decouples your observation from the specific viscoelastic model of the Sharpa Deform Map and substitutes a geometry-only proxy that you can calibrate from mesh data.

**Concrete Isaac Lab changes:**
- In `env_cfg.py`, switch tactile observation source from `contact_force_net` to a new `fingertip_sdf_distance` term computed via batched `robot.forward_kinematics()` against object mesh SDF. Set array resolution to match Sharpa Deform Map output shape (so the student encoder input shape is unchanged).
- Add three new DR parameters to `domain_rand_cfg`: `backlash_deg = Uniform(0.0, 0.8)`, `torque_vel_saturation_scale = Uniform(0.85, 1.0)`, `stiction_nm = Uniform(0.0, 0.15)`. These are currently absent from your Isaac Lab DR config.
- Run the autotune system-ID loop (collect ~200 real rollouts, minimise FK-predicted vs. measured joint trajectory MSE) and report this MSE as your Q3(a) quantification metric. Target: MSE < 0.8° on all 22 joints before starting sim2real deployment.
- Set PPO clip ratio to `ε = 0.1` (not 0.2) for the contact-force-tracking teacher — this paper found tighter clipping reduces reward hacking on the force-tracking objective.

### (d) Failure mode to probe first
The parallel-FK tactile proxy works only if object geometry is well-estimated in real time. **If your object pose estimator drifts by more than ~3mm during in-hand rotation (common with occlusion), the FK distances will be corrupted and the policy will receive systematically wrong tactile observations.** Probe: inject 3mm / 5mm / 10mm positional noise into the object pose observation during eval and measure how quickly the policy fails. If it fails under 5mm noise, your pose estimator is the bottleneck, not the tactile model.

## Paper 3 — PRIORITY ★★

**Tendon Force Modeling for Sim2Real Transfer of RL Policies for Tendon-Driven Robots**
[arXiv:2603.04351](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04351) · Submitted 4 Mar 2026 · Real-robot validation ✓ · EPFL CREATE Lab

**Relevance** Q3 (system-ID / real2sim), Q6 (cross-hardware generalisation)
**Source pass** Keyword Sweep (within 8-week Citation-Graph window)

### (a) Q# / H# touched
- **Q3(b):** System-ID workflow for tendon-driven actuation — directly relevant to the custom hand (spec TBD, but tendon layout is explicitly flagged in §1).
- **Q6:** Method claims robot-agnosticism — the force estimator generalises across different servo motors and tendon configurations.
- **H3 partial:** Identifies a low-dimensional physical parameter (tendon tension curve = f(motor position, contact history)) as the dominant extrinsic to model — consistent with H3's claim that the informative content of RMA extrinsics lives in a low-dimensional physical subspace.

### (b) What is genuinely new
Prior tendon sim2real work (FRoGGeR, various DAPG papers) assumes an analytic or lookup-table torque model. This paper learns a **transformer-based contextual history model** for tendon force from a *new data collection test bench*: a spring-mass-damper system that can apply controlled contact loads directly to a real tendon-driven finger. Key numbers:

- Force prediction error: **< 3% of maximum motor force** (robot-agnostic, validated on two servo types)
- Sim2Real trajectory gap reduction: **41%** (measured as joint position MSE on held-out trajectories)
- Fingertip pose tracking improvement: **50%** vs. baseline analytic model
- Runs inside a GPU-accelerated tendon-force-driven rigid body sim — compatible with Isaac Lab's tendon DOF mode

The transformer uses a **history buffer of motor encoder signals only** — no force sensors at inference. This is the same observability constraint as your Sharpa Hand, which has encoders but no inline tendon load cells.

### (c) Decision implication — what changes in YOUR Isaac Lab pipeline next week if this paper is right

If your custom hand uses tendon-driven actuation (high probability given §1's mention of tendon layout as TBD), this paper's approach is **the most concrete system-ID protocol available** for narrowing your DR ranges before you even have the custom hand hardware.

**Concrete Isaac Lab changes:**
- Before custom hand arrives: build the test bench (spring-mass + load cell + one finger of your custom hand or Sharpa proxy) to collect 2–4 hours of motor encoder + tendon force data under contact and free-movement conditions.
- Train the transformer force estimator offline. Integrate it as a **learned actuator model** in Isaac Lab's `ActuatorCfg` (replacing the default `ImplicitActuatorCfg`) — Isaac Lab supports custom actuator models via `ActuatorBase`.
- **DR tightening protocol:** run the tendon estimator predictions on your real-hand data, then set your DR ranges for `tendon_stiffness` and `tendon_damping` to ±2σ of the residuals from the estimator's predictions (rather than the current wide uniform ranges).
- Metric target: < 5° fingertip pose RMSE across all 22 DOF after integration.

### (d) Failure mode to probe first
The model is validated on a **single-finger test rig** in the paper. A 22-DOF hand with coupled tendon routing (where one motor's tension affects adjacent fingers) introduces cross-finger coupling that the single-finger transformer cannot see. **Probe: measure the force estimator's accuracy on finger 3 when finger 2 is simultaneously loaded.** If accuracy degrades by more than 15%, you need a multi-finger training dataset before this approach is usable on the full Sharpa hand.

## Paper 4 — PRIORITY ★★

**SMP: Abstracting Robot Manipulation Skills via Mixture-of-Experts Diffusion Policies**
[arXiv:2601.21251](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.21251) · Submitted 29 Jan 2026 · Real-robot validation ✓

**Relevance** Q4 (Primitive Skill MoE + online adaptation), H4 (orthogonal skill basis adapts few-shot faster)
**Source pass** Citation-Graph (cited by MoDE-VLA as prior work on skill routing)

### (a) Q# / H# touched
- **Q4 directly:** The paper is a structural experiment on the router-based MoE vs. orthogonal skill basis question asked in Q4.
- **H4 explicitly probed:** SMP implements exactly the "orthogonal, state-adaptive latent basis" architecture H4 predicts will win. The paper validates it against concat-style MoE (Sparse Diffusion Policy) and unstructured MoE on bimanual tasks.

### (b) What is genuinely new
SMP introduces three design decisions that are jointly novel:

1. **Locally-whitened action basis:** At each timestep, the current state determines an orthonormal transformation of the action space. Experts are trained to be orthogonal *in this transformed space*, not globally. This means the "skill decomposition" adapts to the current contact geometry — a re-orientation phase and a translation phase get genuinely different bases, not just different network weights.

2. **Sticky routing with variational objective:** Gates change slowly (low flip-rate), enforced by a KL penalty on gate entropy over time. This is structurally different from token-based MoE (e.g., Switch Transformer), where routing is independent per timestep. Sticky gates produce phase-consistent, non-oscillating behavior — critical for contact-rich manipulation where rapid mode switches cause slip.

3. **Adaptive expert activation at inference:** Only a subset of experts is activated, reducing inference cost vs. dense baselines. The policy still achieves higher success rates than RDT (10× parameters).

**Real-robot evidence:** Bimanual dual-arm platform, multi-task transfer learning. SMP achieves higher success and faster few-shot adaptation than DP, DP3, ACT, and RDT.

**What it does NOT validate:** (a) RL training (SMP is IL/diffusion, not PPO), (b) sim2real transfer, (c) dexterous in-hand contact-rich tasks beyond bimanual pick-and-place. The skills it discovers are arm-level (left/right arm, reach/grasp/release phases), not finger-level (re-orientation, in-hand rotation, controlled release).

### (c) Decision implication — what changes in YOUR Isaac Lab pipeline next week if this paper is right

The implication is for Q4's architecture design, not for an immediate PPO change. SMP is IL-based, so you cannot adopt it directly in your PPO pipeline. However, the **routing design** is directly relevant to your Q4 MoE design:

**Concrete action:** When you implement the Q4 MoE for primitive skills, implement the **locally-whitened router** as an experiment branch alongside the standard concat-MoE baseline. Specifically:
- Add a `compute_local_whitening(state_obs)` function that returns an orthonormal basis W(s) ∈ R^{22×22} via a small MLP (< 256 params).
- Transform the PPO policy's action output `a` to `W(s)^T a` before adding expert residuals.
- Track the gate flip-rate as a diagnostic metric — if flip-rate > 0.3 per step, sticky gating is failing (add temporal entropy KL penalty to the PPO loss).
- H4 predicts this converges faster in few-shot adaptation — measure time-to-80% on a held-out task after 100 real rollouts.

### (d) Failure mode to probe first
SMP's orthogonal basis is computed from **arm-level EE actions**. Your task involves 22-DOF finger-level actions where the relevant skill decomposition is not spatial (left/right arm) but **temporal and contact-topological** (which fingers are in contact, which are moving). **Probe: compute the singular values of the learned action basis W(s) on your in-hand rotation task. If the top eigenvalues don't cluster into interpretable finger groups (thumb vs. non-thumb, contact vs. free), the whitening is not capturing relevant structure, and simple concat-MoE will perform similarly.**

## Paper 5 — CROSS-POLLINATION (Month D: Tactile sensing, adjacent field)

**A-SLIP: Acoustic Sensing for Continuous In-Hand Slip Estimation**
[arXiv:2604.08528](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08528) · Submitted 9 Apr 2026 · Real-robot validation ✓ · CMU Robotics Institute

**Note:** A-SLIP uses a parallel-jaw gripper — normally anti-topic. Included under §8 Cross-Pollination (Month D: tactile sensing / novel sensing modalities). The sensing *principle* — not the hardware — is what transfers.

### (a) Q# / H# touched
- **Q5 (adjacent):** A-SLIP demonstrates that a lightweight acoustic sensor can jointly predict slip **direction and magnitude** continuously at 100 Hz, with < 5mm slip estimation error — using a **lightweight CNN on log-mel spectrograms** of contact-induced vibrations. No optical path, no large sensor footprint.

### (b) What is genuinely new
The Sharpa Deform Map provides a continuous-valued deformation field — it captures what the surface looks like under pressure, but it does not directly detect *slip onset* or *slip direction* in real time. A-SLIP shows that acoustic channels (piezoelectric microphones behind a silicone contact pad) give slip information that is **orthogonal to deformation magnitude**: you can have large deformation without slip (static gripping of a deformable object) and small deformation with slip (smooth-surface object beginning to slide). This is information your current sensor does not provide directly.

The model is 3-channel log-mel + 4-layer CNN. It runs at 100 Hz on an embedded processor. The sensor costs < $50 per fingertip.

### (c) Decision implication
Not an immediate pipeline change. Flag for the custom hand hardware spec (§10, Intentional Gaps): consider adding one acoustic channel per fingertip on the custom hand, alongside the tactile modality. If slip onset prediction runs at 100 Hz as a reactive signal, it could gate the PPO policy's action output (pause → regrasp) without requiring a full policy re-query. This is a lightweight shim between the RL policy and the hardware controller.

### (d) Failure mode to probe first
A-SLIP's slip estimation degrades when object surface texture is very smooth (glass, polished metal). **Probe: test A-SLIP-style acoustic signals on the Sharpa fingertip contact pad against three surface classes — rubber, ABS plastic, and smooth aluminum — and measure slip onset detection accuracy.** If accuracy drops below 70% on smooth metal, acoustic slip sensing adds limited value over the deformation field for the target object set.

## Week-over-Week Synthesis

**Running theme this week:** The sim2real community is converging on a common message — you do not need physics-perfect contact simulation if you correctly model the **actuation chain** (tendon forces, motor current → torque, backlash) and provide a geometry-based tactile proxy rather than a viscoelastic model. Papers 2 and 3 are independent confirmations of this view from different groups (ByteDance Seed, EPFL).

**H2 update (neutral):** No paper this week either strongly supports or challenges H2 (reward-hacking-resistant curriculum > automatic reward synthesis). The Eureka/DrEureka side of this debate was quiet this week. The SMP result is consistent with H2 (the skill structure that actually wins comes from careful routing design, not from scaling or reward synthesis), but it operates in a different regime (IL, not RL). Status: H2 remains unprobed.

**H6 update (weakly supportive):** CGP's contact-consistency-loss architecture is a direct implementation of H6's prediction. The fact that it outperforms raw-concatenation baselines on an Allegro hand is weakly supportive. However, CGP is IL-based (diffusion policy), not RL-based — whether the same contact-consistency structure provides equivalent benefits in a PPO sim2real pipeline is untested.
